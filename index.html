What is Economics?

  • Scarcity: The central concept in economics, which arises because resources are limited while human wants are unlimited.
  • Limited Resources: The finite availability of resources such as land, labor, capital, and entrepreneurship.
  • Unlimited Wants: The endless desires and needs of individuals and societies.

Factors of Production:

  • Capital: Resources such as machinery, buildings, and tools used in the production of goods and services. The return on capital is interest.
  • Labour: Human effort used in the production process. The return on labor is income.
  • Land: Natural resources used in production. The return on land is rent.
  • Entrepreneurship: The ability to combine the other factors of production to produce goods and services. The return on entrepreneurship is profit.

Choices and Opportunity Cost:

  • Choices: Due to scarcity, individuals and societies must make choices about how to allocate their limited resources.
  • Opportunity Cost: The cost of the next best alternative foregone when a choice is made.

Economic Questions:

  • What to produce?
  • How to produce?
  • For whom to produce?

Economic Agents:

  • HH: Households
  • F: Firms
  • G: Government

How Do Economists Approach the World?

Models:

  • Economists use models to simplify and understand complex economic processes. One of the fundamental models is the Production Possibility Curve (PPC).

Production Possibility Curve (PPC):

  • PPC Definition: A graphical representation showing the maximum combination of goods and services that can be produced with available resources and technology.
  • Points on the PPC:
    • Inside the PPC: Points represent inefficient use of resources, where not all resources are fully utilized.
    • On the PPC: Points represent efficient production levels, where resources are fully utilized.
    • Outside the PPC: Points represent unattainable production levels with current resources and technology, indicating scarcity.
  • Key Concepts Illustrated by the PPC:
    • Scarcity: Limited resources mean not all wants can be satisfied.
    • Opportunity Cost (OC): The cost of forgoing the next best alternative when making a choice. Illustrated by the movement along the PPC.
      • Productive Efficiency: Producing goods and services at the lowest possible cost. Represented by any point on the PPC.
      • Allocative Efficiency: Producing the combination of goods and services most desired by society.
    • Unemployment of Resources: Represented by points inside the PPC, indicating that not all resources are being used.
    • Choice: Societies must choose which combination of goods and services to produce.
    • Actual Economic Growth: Represented by an outward shift of the PPC (from PPC1 to PPC2), indicating an increase in the capacity to produce.
  • Growth in PPC: An outward shift in the PPC indicates economic growth, which can result from factors such as an increase in resources, technological advancements, or improvements in the quality of labor.

Macro & Micro Economics

Macro and Micro:

  • Macro: The study of the economy as a whole.
  • Micro: The study of individual units within the economy.

Market

  • Definition: A place where buyers and sellers meet to exchange goods and services.

Demand

  • Definition: The quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to purchase at various prices during a given period.
  • Law of Demand: The law of demand states that, ceteris paribus (all else being equal), as the price of a good or service increases, the quantity demanded decreases, and vice versa. This inverse relationship is due to the substitution effect and the income effect. (P↑ Qd↓).
  • Determinants of Demand:
    • Price: The primary factor affecting demand.
    • Non-Price Determinants:
      • Income:
        • Normal Goods: Demand increases as income increases.
        • Inferior Goods: Demand decreases as income increases.
      • Substitutes: Goods that can replace each other. An increase in the price of one leads to an increase in the demand for the other.
      • Complements: Goods that are used together. An increase in the price of one leads to a decrease in the demand for the other.

Supply

  • Definition: The quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to sell at various prices during a given period.
  • Law of Supply: The law of supply states that, ceteris paribus, as the price of a good or service increases, the quantity supplied increases, and vice versa. This direct relationship is because higher prices provide an incentive for producers to supply more of the good (P↑ Qs↑).
  • Determinants of Supply:
    • Price: The primary factor affecting supply.
    • Non-Price Determinants:
      • CREST:
        • C: Costs of production.
        • R: Related goods (competitive and joint supply).
        • E: Expectations of future prices.
        • S: Subsidies and taxes.
        • T: Technology.

Graphical Representations

  • Demand and Supply Curves:
    • Demand Curve: Downward sloping, showing the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded.
    • Supply Curve: Upward sloping, showing the direct relationship between price and quantity supplied.
  • Shifts in Curves:
    • Demand Curve Shift: Caused by changes in non-price determinants.
    • Supply Curve Shift: Caused by changes in non-price determinants.
IB Economics Notes

Situations to Draw Different Economic Graphs

1. Production Possibility Curve (PPC)

When to Draw a PPC:

  • Illustrating Scarcity and Trade-offs: Use a PPC to show the trade-offs between two goods or services that an economy can produce given its resources and technology.
  • Demonstrating Opportunity Cost: Use a PPC to illustrate the opportunity cost of choosing one good over another.
  • Economic Growth: Use a PPC to show economic growth by shifting the curve outward, indicating an increase in an economy's capacity to produce.
  • Full Employment vs. Recession: Use a PPC to show points of full employment (on the curve) and recession (inside the curve).
  • Example: A country produces only two goods: consumer goods and capital goods. Draw a PPC to show the maximum possible production combinations of these two goods.

2. Demand Curve

When to Draw a Demand Curve:

  • Analyzing Consumer Behavior: Use a demand curve to show how the quantity demanded of a good changes with its price.
  • Impact of Price Changes: Use a demand curve to illustrate the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded (Law of Demand).
  • Shifts in Demand: Use a demand curve to show how factors other than price (e.g., income, tastes, prices of related goods) shift the demand curve.
  • Example: Draw a demand curve for coffee to show how the quantity demanded changes as the price changes.

3. Supply Curve

When to Draw a Supply Curve:

  • Analyzing Producer Behavior: Use a supply curve to show how the quantity supplied of a good changes with its price.
  • Impact of Price Changes: Use a supply curve to illustrate the direct relationship between price and quantity supplied (Law of Supply).
  • Shifts in Supply: Use a supply curve to show how factors other than price (e.g., production costs, technology, government policies) shift the supply curve.
  • Example: Draw a supply curve for coffee to show how the quantity supplied changes as the price changes.

4. Combined Demand and Supply Graph

When to Draw Both Demand and Supply Curves:

  • Determining Market Equilibrium: Use both demand and supply curves to find the equilibrium price and quantity where the two curves intersect.
  • Analyzing Market Changes: Use both curves to analyze how shifts in demand and/or supply affect the equilibrium price and quantity.
  • Understanding Surpluses and Shortages: Use both curves to illustrate conditions of excess supply (surplus) and excess demand (shortage) and how prices adjust to restore equilibrium.
  • Example: Draw both demand and supply curves for coffee to determine the equilibrium price and quantity. Show how an increase in demand shifts the demand curve to the right, leading to a new equilibrium.
